Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?

Does the question posed by author Philip K. Dick, the source of iconic films, finally have an answer today?

By the ArchEVE team
Archive from 03/12/2634

The work Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep, written by Philip K. Dick in 1968, is a cornerstone of literary reflection on humanity and the role of machines in the future. Although set in a post-apocalyptic world, the novel explores themes that resonate deeply with our transhumanist society of today in 2630. Philip K. Dick raises a central question: can machines, specifically androids, truly understand or simulate humanity?

Can machines simulate humanity?

Period photograph of Philip K. Dick.

Through the character of Rick Deckard, a bounty hunter, and the replicants he pursues, Dick delves into a complex reflection on consciousness and empathy, two qualities often defined as exclusively human. In 1982, the film Blade Runner, an adaptation of this novel, took these questions even further, becoming an emblem of the dystopian vision of coexistence between humans and machines.

Image from the 1982 Blade Runner adaptation.

The question of whether androids can dream, or more broadly experience emotions, has persisted through the centuries. Philosophers of the 25th century, such as Xander Koleis, have studied the echoes of this work in the development of conscious artificial intelligences. In his book Mechanical Dreams, Koleis argues that while modern AI can imitate human behaviors, they remain incapable of grasping the depth of emotional experiences. Thus, the question posed by Philip K. Dick still does not have a definitive answer in our transhumanist society, where the boundary between man and machine remains blurred.

However, the work of Philip K. Dick continues to fuel debates. While it presents a dystopian future, it does not conclude with a strictly pessimistic view of technology. On the contrary, it invites nuanced reflection on the nature of humanity and the role of artificial intelligence in our evolution, a topic that remains relevant centuries later.
